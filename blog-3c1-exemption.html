Expanding 3(c)(1) for Small Venture Capital Funds: Why It Matters

March 12, 2025

As seed investments balloon and more founders emerge from every corner of the world, small venture capital (VC) funds have never been more vital in shaping the future of innovation. Yet many first-time or smaller-scale fund managers are finding it harder to operate under the current limits of Section 3(c)(1) of the Investment Company Act. That's where the new legislative proposal, the Improving Capital Allocation for Newcomers (ICAN) Act, comes in. Below, we break down what's changing, why it's crucial for early-stage capital, and how it could energize our entire startup ecosystem.

The Current Hurdles for Small VC Funds

Under Section 3(c)(1), privately offered funds can skip registering as "investment companies"—a regulatory relief that helps smaller players get off the ground. But even after the SEC's 2023 adjustment (raising the exemption threshold from $10 million to $12 million), small funds still face two big constraints:

  1. Investor Count Cap – Funds can have fewer than 250 accredited investors, limiting the ability to pool many smaller checks.
  2. Capital Limit – The $12 million threshold hasn't kept pace with bigger seed rounds and rising valuations, leaving smaller funds short on diversification.

For new and diverse fund managers, this is especially tough. They often raise from a broader base of smaller investors—yet the 250-person limit and modest capital cap can stall momentum before they really begin.

The Proposed Fix: ICAN Act

To address these barriers, policymakers have introduced the ICAN Act—a legislative update to Section 3(c)(1) specifically for small venture capital funds. Its two major moves:

  • Boost the beneficial-owner limit from 250 to 2,000 for qualified small VC funds.
  • Raise the fund size cap from $12 million to $150 million.

Crucially, the Act keeps accredited-investor requirements and other investor safeguards intact—modernizing, not removing, core protections.

Why This Matters: More Capital, More Opportunity

  1. Broader Access – Up to 2,000 accredited investors means a bigger pool of supporters, especially those writing smaller checks.
  2. Greater Inclusion – First-time and underrepresented fund managers often rely on large networks of smaller backers.
  3. Fuel for Underserved Geographies – Startups outside major tech hubs can benefit from local or regional funds that have more room to grow.
  4. Stronger Startup Formation – More capital fosters competition, creativity, and job creation.

Maintaining Investor Protections

The ICAN Act still requires accredited investor status and upholds anti-fraud provisions and fiduciary duties. In other words, it's about updating the rules to match the realities of 2025, not discarding them.
